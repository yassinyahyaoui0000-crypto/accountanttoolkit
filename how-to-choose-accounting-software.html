How to Choose Accounting Software as a Freelance Accountant

Choosing accounting software for your own practice is a different problem than choosing it for a client. You already understand the accounting — what you need to figure out is which tool fits your specific workflow, client volume, and budget without overcompleting the job. This guide cuts through the noise and gives you a practical framework for making the right call.

The mistake most freelancers make

Most freelance accountants choose their practice software the same way they recommend software to clients — by evaluating features. They build a spreadsheet, compare feature lists, and pick the tool with the most checkboxes ticked.

The problem is that a solo practitioner with 8 clients has fundamentally different needs than a small business with 15 employees. Feature depth matters less than workflow fit. A tool you'll actually use consistently is worth more than a theoretically superior tool you find frustrating to open.

The right question isn't "which tool has the most features?" It's "which tool reduces my admin time the most for my specific situation?"

A five-step decision framework

1

Count your active clients and monthly transactions

Under 5 clients with simple billing? A free tool handles this comfortably. 10+ clients with recurring retainers, varying scopes, and monthly reporting? You need something with automation and depth. Be honest about where you are now and where you realistically expect to be in 12 months.

2

Decide whether you need double-entry accounting

As an accountant, you know what double-entry means and why it matters. The question is whether you need it for your own practice books. If you're tracking simple income and expenses for a solo operation, single-entry is adequate. If you want accurate financial statements for your own practice management, double-entry is the right choice.

3

Identify your biggest time drain

Is it chasing invoices? Reconciling transactions? Onboarding new clients? Each tool has different automation strengths. Zoho Books excels at workflow automation. Bonsai eliminates client onboarding friction. Xero leads on bank reconciliation speed. Match the tool's strength to your biggest pain point.

4

Check your integration requirements

Do you use specific tools that need to connect to your accounting software? A receipt scanner, a CRM, a time tracker, a payroll tool? List them before choosing. QuickBooks integrates with 750+ tools. Zoho Books connects primarily within the Zoho ecosystem. Xero sits in the middle with strong third-party integrations.

5

Set a monthly budget ceiling

What's the maximum you're willing to spend per month on practice management software? $15? $40? $60? This immediately eliminates some options and clarifies others. A solo practitioner billing $2,000/month should not be spending $100/month on software — that's 5% of revenue before expenses.

Features that actually matter vs features that don't

Software vendors market every feature as essential. Here's a more honest breakdown for a freelance accountant's own practice:

Essential for most practitioners

  • Recurring invoice automation
  • Bank feed connection
  • Automatic payment reminders
  • Basic financial reports (P&L, balance sheet)
  • Mobile receipt capture
  • Client portal for invoice viewing

Nice to have — not essential

  • Advanced inventory tracking
  • Multi-currency (unless international clients)
  • Payroll processing
  • Built-in contracts (use a separate tool)
  • Project profitability analysis
  • Custom report builder
Watch out for: Tools that charge extra for features that should be standard — like automatic payment reminders, client portals, or bank connections. These are table stakes in 2025, not premium add-ons.

How to evaluate during a free trial

Most tools offer 14–30 day free trials. The mistake is signing up and passively clicking around. To make a useful evaluation, simulate your actual workflow during the trial period:

  • Create invoices for your 3 most common billing scenarios
  • Set up a recurring invoice for a retainer client
  • Connect your business bank account and reconcile the last month
  • Generate a P&L report and check it looks correct
  • Try capturing a receipt on mobile and verify it categorizes correctly
  • Time how long each task takes — this is your real benchmark

The 30-minute test: If you can't set up a functional invoice, connect your bank, and generate a basic report within 30 minutes of signing up, the tool has a usability problem. Good accounting software for a solo practitioner should be up and running quickly.

When to switch tools

Switching accounting software mid-practice is painful — data migration, re-learning a new interface, potential reconciliation issues. The cost of switching is high enough that it's worth being deliberate about your initial choice. That said, there are clear signals that a switch is warranted:

  • You're spending more than 3 hours per week on admin tasks that should be automated
  • Your client volume has doubled and the tool is hitting limits you didn't expect
  • You need integrations your current tool doesn't support
  • You've outgrown single-entry accounting and need double-entry
  • The monthly cost has become disproportionate to what you're getting

If you're hitting these signals, switch sooner rather than later — the longer you wait, the more historical data you'll need to migrate.

Your pre-purchase checklist

Before committing to any tool, verify:

  • I've counted my active clients and monthly invoice volume
  • I've decided whether I need double-entry accounting
  • I've identified my biggest current admin time drain
  • I've listed all integrations I need with other tools
  • I've set a monthly budget ceiling
  • I've signed up for at least one free trial and used it with real data
  • I've timed how long basic tasks take in the trial
  • I've checked that the tool exports data in a usable format (for future migration)
  • I've confirmed payment processing fees if I'll collect money through the tool
  • I've read at least 10 recent user reviews from people in a similar situation
