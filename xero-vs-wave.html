Both Xero and Wave are popular choices for freelance accountants — but they're built for very different situations. One charges a monthly fee and gives you a genuinely powerful platform. What we're comparing

Xero and Wave both serve freelance accountants and bookkeepers, but the comparison almost isn't fair — they target different stages of a practice. Xero is a professional-grade accounting platform used by over 3 million businesses worldwide. Wave is a free tool that covers the basics and makes money through payment processing fees instead of subscriptions.

We evaluated both tools specifically from the perspective of a freelance accountant or bookkeeper managing their own practice — not a small business owner using accounting software for their shop. The features that matter most to you are different: client management, invoicing flexibility, reporting depth, and how well the software handles the slightly unusual position of being both the accountant and the client.

Pricing: the honest breakdown

Xero pricing

Xero runs on a tiered subscription model. The Starter plan at $15/month is deliberately limited — you're capped at 20 invoices and 5 bills per month, which is too restrictive for most active freelancers. The Standard plan at $42/month removes those limits and is where most solo practitioners land. The Premium plan adds multi-currency support.

One thing worth knowing: Xero frequently offers promotional pricing for the first 6 months, so it's worth checking their current offers before signing up.

Wave pricing

Wave's accounting, invoicing, and receipt scanning are genuinely free — no trial period, no hidden fees for the core product. The catch is that payment processing costs extra: 2.9% + $0.60 per credit card transaction, and bank payments cost 1% (minimum $1). If you're collecting payments through Wave, those fees add up. If you just use it for bookkeeping and send invoices paid via bank transfer, it stays free.

Invoicing: where they differ most

For a freelance accountant, invoicing is one of the most used features — you're billing clients regularly, often with recurring retainer arrangements.

Xero invoicing

Xero's invoicing is genuinely excellent. You can create professional, branded invoices in minutes, set up automatic recurring invoices for retainer clients, and send automatic payment reminders. The online payment integration means clients can pay directly from the invoice via credit card or direct debit. For a freelance accountant billing 10–20 clients per month, the automation alone is worth the subscription cost.

Wave invoicing

Wave's invoicing is surprisingly capable for a free tool. You get customizable templates, recurring invoices, automatic payment reminders, and online payment collection. It covers everything most solo practitioners need. The main limitation is the payment processing fees if you collect card payments — for high-volume billing, these fees can exceed what Xero's subscription would cost.

The math: If you process $5,000/month in client payments via Wave's card processing (2.9% + $0.60/transaction), you're paying roughly $150–$175/month in fees. Xero at $42/month looks very different in that context.

Bank reconciliation

This is where Xero pulls decisively ahead. Bank reconciliation in Xero is fast, intelligent, and built for accountants. Xero connects to most major banks directly, imports transactions automatically, and uses machine learning to suggest matches that get smarter over time. For a freelance accountant managing their own books, it genuinely reduces reconciliation time significantly.

Wave's bank reconciliation works but feels more manual. It connects to banks via Plaid, which works well for US accounts but can be unreliable for some international banks. The matching suggestions are less sophisticated than Xero's. It gets the job done for simple accounts, but if you have multiple income streams or high transaction volume, it becomes tedious.

Reporting and insights

Xero's reporting suite is comprehensive — profit and loss, balance sheet, cash flow, aged receivables, custom reports. As a freelance accountant, you already know how to read these and Xero produces them correctly. Reports are exportable to PDF and Excel and can be shared directly with a link.

Wave's reporting covers the basics — P&L, balance sheet, sales tax reports — but it's noticeably thinner. There's no cash flow forecast, no customizable reports, and limited filtering options. For your own simple practice finances, it's adequate. For anything more complex, you'll hit the ceiling quickly.

Our verdict: which should you choose?

The honest answer is that this depends entirely on where you are in your freelance journey — not on which tool is objectively "better."

The right tool for your situation

X
Choose Xero if you have an established practice You're billing 5+ clients regularly, need proper reconciliation, want time-saving automation, or have international clients. The $42/month pays for itself in saved admin time within weeks.
W
Choose Wave if you're just starting out You have 1–4 clients, want zero overhead while you find your footing, and don't need multi-currency or deep reporting. Upgrade to Xero when you're billing consistently.
!
Don't use Wave if you have international clients Wave has no multi-currency support whatsoever. If any of your clients pay in a different currency, go straight to Xero Premium or consider alternatives like FreshBooks.

Alternatives worth considering

If neither feels like the right fit, two other tools are worth looking at for freelance accountants specifically:

  • FreshBooks — better invoicing and time tracking than either Xero or Wave, but weaker on the accounting side. Good if billing and client management are your priority.
  • Bonsai — built specifically for freelancers, includes contracts and proposals alongside invoicing. Less accounting depth but excellent for the full client workflow.
